Only those close to transgender children and teens realize the distress that having their birth name and/or gender marker on school paperwork can cause. I\u2019ve seen the panic in their eyes firsthand. They are often petrified of having their peers learn their birth name. They are worried about being \u201couted\u201d. The following is an excerpt from Guidance for Massachusetts Public Schools: Creating a Safe and Supportive School Environment; Nondiscrimination on the Basis of Gender Identity: \u201cThe 2011 National School Climate Survey by the Gay, Lesbian & Straight Education Network (GLSEN), found that 75.4% of transgender students had been verbally harassed in the previous year, 32.1% had been physically harassed, and 16.8% had been physically assaulted. A court-ordered name or gender change is not required, and the student need not change his or her official records. The intentional or persistent refusal to respect a student\u2019s gender identity (for example, intentionally referring to the student by a name or pronoun that does not correspond to the student\u2019s gender identity) is a violation of this policy.\u201d The following are excerpts from California School Boards Association (CSBA)\u2019s \u201cFinal Guidance: AB1266, Transgender and Gender Nonconforming Students, Privacy, Programs, Activities and Facilities\u201c: \u201cUpon request, districts should prepare data systems to list a transgender or gender nonconforming student by his or her preferred name and gender.\u201d \u201cPrivacy Rights of Transgender or Gender Nonconforming Student  A student\u2019s decision to inform the district that his or her gender identity differs from his or her biological gender is extremely personal and private. At the same time, the decision may potentially involve very public components if, for example, the student starts to go by a different name.

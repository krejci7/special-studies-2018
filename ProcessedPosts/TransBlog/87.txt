That scene may seem like pure comedy, but like all comedy it exaggerates a real and often painful aspect of our lives: identity stress.  We all \u2013 trans people and everyone else \u2013 take on multiple roles in our lives, some gendered, some not.  Sometimes you\u2019re the teacher, sometimes the student.  Sometimes you\u2019re the artist, sometimes the subject.  And often you\u2019re nobody in particular, just a person on the street. Each of those roles comes with different standards of behavior and the expectation of different treatment, and that can be more stressful than the different clothing that is sometimes expected.  When someone who is used to being treated as a Very Important Person is confronted with the expectations of ordinary people, like getting pulled over for speeding or having to wait on line, a common response is, \u201cDo you know who I am?\u201d This identity stress can be particularly acute for trans people, or anyone who takes transgender actions, whether they identify as trans or not.  About eight years ago, Norah Vincent wrote a fascinating book called Self-Made Man, where she presented as a man called Ned and participated in a series of male-dominated activities such as competitive bowling and hard selling.  She personally identifies as a non-trans lesbian and never had any intention to transition, but she felt what I call transgender feelings: a desire to be a man in order to escape some of the burden of her gender and partake in male privilege.

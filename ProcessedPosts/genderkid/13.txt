No need for a judge\u2019s permission, no need for psychiatric approval, no need for any body modifications. \u2013People who do want to modify their body will have the right to have hormones and surgery covered by their health insurance (or by the public health system, if they\u2019re uninsured). \u2013There\u2019s a special clause to protect the rights of trans youths: if a kid\u2019s parents were unsupportive, a judge would intervene in the best interest of the child. Just a few years ago, when I started transitioning, it was impossible to change your ID without genital surgery. During the 90\u2019s, many trans women and travestis suffered and died due to police persecution. The statistics for trans people \u2013unemployment, school drop-out rate, life expectancy\u2013 are still dismal. It goes to show that countries can change, though legal rights are just the beginning. Things can get better when there\u2019s a large movement willing to take on the fight. That\u2019s another thing I love about this law. It doesn\u2019t put trans people in a passive place of suffering, illness or victimization. It wasn\u2019t written by doctors, psychiatrists or condescending allies. The text of the law was written largely by the trans movement. The very process of passing this bill acknowledged the trans community as a force to be reckoned with. All the large LGB organizations backed the law, but trans leaders and organizations were at the heart of the process. I find that very empowering. It\u2019s a shift from a place of despair to a place of anger.

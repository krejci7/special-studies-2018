And, when these two incidents happened, I still had long hair. Both situations were almost the same: we were at the house of my parents\u2019 friends, and the kid of the family pulled me down to their height. \u201cAre you a boy or a girl?\u201d they each whispered into my ear. \u201cI\u2019m a girl.\u201d \u201cOh.\u201d Pause. \u201cThen why aren\u2019t you wearing earrings?\u201d or, \u201cWhy aren\u2019t you wearing a dress?\u201d I think I could have answered \u201cI\u2019m a boy\u201d to these kids, and they would have totally gotten it. Another issue that interests me is how parents deal with gendering their kids, especially when the parents are cool and totally gender-savvy. The problem is, how is the rest of the world going to treat non-stereotypically-gendered children? I don\u2019t know too much about kids, but several writers have tackled this issue:  Blue milk (a mother) has recent posts about three-year-olds discussing queer politics and pink for boys.
